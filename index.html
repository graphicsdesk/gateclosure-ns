More than a year into gate closures, West Harlem residents feel excluded from Columbia

At the forefront of athletic safety, the Ivy League’s regulation changes dating back to 2016 have made attempts to reduce concussions.

By Isha Banerjee and Skyler Gluck

November 21, 2024

Anna Fedorova / Senior Staff Photographer

For 40 years, Harry Schwartz, CC ’56, has walked through Columbia’s Morningside campus to get to the 116th Street and Broadway subway station from his apartment on Morningside Drive. After finding out last fall that he would lose access to College Walk, Schwartz, who is 89 years old and has a physical disability, said he has effectively stopped using the station.

Spectator spoke to four community leaders, three businesses on Amsterdam Avenue, and three other residents about the impact of campus closures on the University’s surrounding community. Some residents expressed frustration about extended commute times and the inaccessiblity of campus green spaces, while community leaders said the lack of direct communication and consultation further divides Columbia from West Harlem.

Victor Edwards, chair of Community Board 9, said that he has not received any communication from the University regarding gate closures.

“It’s just happenstance,” Edwards said. “If you go up there then you’ll find out what’s going on, but no official outreach to the board.”

The University restricted campus access to Columbia ID holders for the first time in 55 years on Oct. 12, 2023, ahead of planned demonstrations over the war in Gaza. Ever since the establishment of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” on April 17, administrators have largely limited campus access, and in July they announced a tiered, color-coded system to indicate campus restriction.

Later in the summer, Columbia reopened its gates to the public from 7 a.m. to 7 p.m. daily, but at the beginning of the academic year access reverted to CUID holders only. Interim University President Katrina Armstrong said she hoped to move toward a fully reopened campus in a “matter of weeks, not months,” in a Sept. 24 email to the Columbia community.

“The University is evaluating Morningside campus access on an ongoing basis as we balance the desire for an open campus that is fully accessible to all of Columbia’s valued constituencies, including our neighbors, with the need for safety and security during this immediate period of uncertainty,” University spokesperson Millie Wert wrote in a statement to Spectator.

Currently, anyone who is not a CUID holder is unable to access the campus unless pre-registered as a guest by a Columbia affiliate 24 hours in advance.

“Gated communities are safe, but they’re not really urban spaces,” Dave Robinson, president of Morningside Heights Community Coalition, said. “They’re just protected fortresses, and that’s kind of the feeling that it creates.”

For Schwartz, who found out by reading the news that he would no longer be allowed on campus, the restricted access, coupled with his disability, makes it difficult to do daily tasks and get to the bus.

“In my particular case, I’m disabled,” Schwartz said. “I have arthritic ankles, I use a cane, and it’s a real hardship for me.”

“Columbia’s commitment to New York City and to our community, as well as the daily experiences of our neighbors, particularly the elderly and disabled, weighs heavily on our decision-making,” Wert wrote. “We have continued to make adjustments to our guest registration process, and we are evaluating additional communication measures to keep neighbors informed. None of these measures is meant to prolong the period of restricted access and they do not diminish our full focus on working toward a fully reopened campus.”

For many community leaders, frustration surrounding gate closures stems from a lack of communication from the University. Three business owners and managers between 115th and 120th streets on Amsterdam Avenue who lease spaces from Columbia told Spectator that the University has not contacted them about the gate closures.

Peter Asan, a manager at the sandwich shop Subsconscious, which has leased their Amsterdam Avenue storefront from Columbia for decades, said the lack of communication is unprecedented.

“The University hasn’t said anything to us. Only we find out through the news or through the students or the staff that what’s going on,” Asan said. “They haven’t reached us, which I’m surprised. Usually Columbia, they let us know what’s going on, what’s going to be happening. But no, nothing.”

Gary Calder, a partner at Village Copier, a print shop on 118th Street and Amsterdam Avenue, said that he finds the lack of campus access and communication to be “absurd,” especially because the store has been a tenant in the neighborhood for over 30 years.

“I’ve asked for reasonings, and I get no answers,” Calder said. “The responses I’ve gotten have not been acceptable. … I’m not the only one that feels this way. I’ve talked to everybody in the neighborhood that’s around here and they feel the same way.”

Calder also emailed Columbia several times to ask for campus access for his team to commute to and from work. According to Calder, the University’s responses were not favorable.

According to Jon Simons, manager of Arts and Crafts Beer Parlor, the campus closures have also affected the bar’s revenue, as well as the commute home for nighttime bartenders. While the bar has tried to communicate with the University, Simons said the efforts have largely been ineffective in gaining access to campus.

“We’ve reached out to several different departments in the University, and we’ve all gotten kicked back that we won’t have access to go through campus ourselves,” Simons said. “A big issue with us is, especially our nighttime bartenders, they’re missing trains because they’ve got to walk four extra blocks around instead of walking through campus to the train and getting home in time.”

Kai Cogsville, the founder of Defend Harlem, said that although the lack of access to campus has “never happened” before, it is reflective of the larger divide between the University and the West Harlem community.

“I think it’s kind of just like a big shock,” Cogsville said. “But it’s always kind of been that there’s a disconnect between the University and members of the community.”

For Dan McSweeney, a member of the Morningside Heights Community Coalition, communication and transparency from the University regarding the community’s access to campus is key.

“We would like to, as neighbors, number one, better understand what the decision-making process is, how these decisions are made,” McSweeney said. “Then, number two, what can be done to mitigate the negative impacts of the campus closure.”

In her Sept. 24 email, Armstrong added that Columbia gains more from its location than any other university.

“Columbia will continue to exist as Columbia only to the degree we remain open to and engaged with the world beyond our gates,” Armstrong wrote. “Indeed, across higher education, no university is more deeply associated with or gains more from its location.”

Emphasizing the University’s relationship to the New York City community, Armstrong wrote that the University is “proudly ‘Columbia University in the City of New York.’”

However, for some members of the community, like Genia Gould, a resident on 116th Street and Claremont Avenue, the closures have upset the balance between the neighborhood and the University.

“There’s a symbiosis where Columbia depends on the businesses, the folks that have been here a long time, because it is New York City, it’s not Columbia City,” Gould said.

Though several community members said they had relied on access to Columbia’s formerly open campus, some community leaders said that other members of the community did not know they were allowed to access Columbia’s campus even before the gate closures.

“There still is the case that a lot of people in this area aren’t even aware of that,” McSweeney said. “They just assume that they’re not allowed on campus in general. So there’s not the level of two-way communication that should exist with such a large institution in the midst of a more diverse neighborhood or community.”

Before the installation of the College Walk gates, 116th Street between Broadway and Amsterdam Avenue was a thoroughfare open to vehicles. In 1953, New York City ceded the rights to the thoroughfare to Columbia for the University’s “bicentennial beautification plans” and closed the street to vehicular traffic. Amid these changes, the University maintained that the walkway would remain open for pedestrians. Two years later, Columbia renamed the street College Walk.

Columbia installed the gates on the 116th Street and Broadway entrance in 1967. The gates, which were a $89,000 gift from the George and Margarita Delacorte Foundation to the University, were meant to be a decorative addition to Columbia’s architecture. The University insisted that the gates would be purely ornamental and would remain open to ensure free access for the public.

The next year, Columbia restricted its gates to CUID holders for the first time following the 1968 student protests against the Vietnam War and the opening of a largely private gymnasium in Morningside Park with separate entrances for University affiliates and Harlem residents.

Robinson said the gate closures do not send “a good message” to the West Harlem neighborhood.

“What it communicates to the community is ‘We are an institution separate from the neighborhood, separate from the city, and are afraid, really, of what is out there,’” Robinson said.

Still, some remain hopeful that campus access will eventually be reinstated. Lesley Weinberg, a resident on 104th Street, said that she felt optimistic that tensions will eventually “settle down” and campus will reopen.

“It’s a loss,” Weinberg said. “But I look at it as a temporary loss that will hopefully return to normal.”

Though Schwartz said residents understand the reasons for the gate closures, he said it is time to reopen the campus for the West Harlem community.

“We understand what produced it,” Schwartz said. “But we think it’s time now to let people who have been using it for years resume that, and let this vital path in the neighborhood be open again.”
